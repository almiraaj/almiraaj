About Power of Attorney Apostille/Attestation

Issuing a Power of Attorney enables someone to represent you legally or financially. When intended for international use, this document must be apostilled or attested to comply with legal standards abroad.

Apostille is recognized by countries under the Hague Convention, while other nations require additional embassy attestation. This process avoids the need for you to travel personally and ensures validity across borders.

Common departments involved before embassy/legal legalization:

  • Notary Public Attestation: Certifies that your document is signed and verified.
  • Home Department: Required for personal/non-educational certificates.
  • SDM (Sub Divisional Magistrate): Done prior to embassy attestation, especially for UAE, Qatar, Kuwait.
  • COC (Chamber of Commerce): Legalizes commercial POAs like trade licenses.
  • MEA Apostille: Validates documents for Hague Convention countries.

Note: Procedures may vary depending on the destination country and its consular policies.

Why This Matters

A Power of Attorney (POA) is more than just a piece of paper—it’s your legal voice when you can’t be physically present. Whether you're setting up a business abroad, managing real estate in another country, or navigating visa paperwork, a legally attested POA ensures your intentions are respected and recognized globally.

Apostille or attestation validates your POA across borders. Without proper legalization, your representative may face rejection at banks, embassies, or government offices overseas. It’s not just a requirement—it’s a safeguard.

  • Empowers someone to manage assets, banking, and legal matters on your behalf.
  • Required by foreign embassies, consulates, and real estate agencies.
  • Accepted by international universities, banks, and judicial authorities.
  • Gives peace of mind and legal clarity for remote transactions.
